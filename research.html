Research in the Sasca Lab

Our research group specializes in employing a diverse range of modern techniques in quantitative and functional genomics, as well as quantitative proteomics, with a strong emphasis on translational implications. Our primary goal is to decipher chromatin dynamics associated with cancer progression and resistance, aiming to identify means for therapeutically targeting new vulnerabilities in cancer. Our work spans across all technical facets, from molecular biology isolation/enrichment methods to library preparation, and extends subsequently to computational data processing, analysis, and integration

Cohesin project


A balanced hematopoiesis is essential to sustain the continuous production of blood cells. Disrupting the equilibrium between self-renewal and differentiation of hematopoietic stem cells (HSCs) leads to hematologic malignancies such as myelodysplastic syndromes (MDS), characterized by uncontrolled HSC self-renewal and impaired lineage differentiation. High-risk MDS often progresses to secondary acute myeloid leukemia (AML) through the expansion of clones carrying leukemic driver mutations. Gene mutations shared between MDS and AML fall into several biological categories. However, the cohesin complex constitutes a distinct group, underscoring its biological significance.

STAG2, the most frequently mutated cohesin subunit, is associated with poor prognosis. Loss of STAG2 in HSCs enhances self-renewal, impairs differentiation, and disrupts enhancer–promoter interactions that control lineage-specific transcription. Because there are currently no mutation-specific therapies, understanding the mechanisms driven by STAG2 loss may reveal candidate targets.

To explore therapeutic strategies for Stag2-deficient hematopoiesis at a pre-malignant stage, we developed an inducible Stag2 knockdown model in HPCs, capable of expanding myelopoiesis under IL-3 stimulation. We integrate ChIP-seq, CUT&RUN, RNA-seq, and RIME proteomics to map cohesin occupancy, enhancer activity, and interacting transcription factors. In parallel, we perform inhibitor screens and validate findings in cohesin-mutant AML lines to identify therapeutic vulnerabilities in STAG2-mutated myeloid neoplasms.

Acute resistance to BETi project


BET inhibitors have shown limited effectiveness in acute myeloid leukemia (AML), largely because AML cells rapidly rewire their transcriptional programs to escape therapeutic pressure. Our project investigates the immediate epigenetic and transcriptional responses that occur right after BET inhibition. We found that releasing BRD4 from chromatin triggers fast compensatory feedback that blunts the intended downregulation of key gene networks—and in some cases even increases their expression. A central mediator of this adaptive response is the epigenetic co-activator p300. We show that p300 activity is required at multiple stages of resistance development, although the specific transcriptional modules it controls vary across AML subtypes. In some AMLs, p300 primarily drives early, transitional transcriptional states that help cells adjust to the initial loss of BET function. In others, p300 maintains a more linear resistance program and remains essential throughout the evolution of resistance.

Together, these findings define how AML cells establish both acute and chronic states of resistance to BET inhibition through p300-dependent mechanisms. Importantly, our data suggest that a sequential treatment strategy combining BET and p300 inhibition may prevent the establishment of resistance and ultimately improve therapeutic efficacy.
